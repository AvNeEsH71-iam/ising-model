% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The two-dimensional Ising model with ferromagnetic nearest-neighbour coupling is
simulated using a Cellular Automata (CA) approach with checkerboard (red--black)
decomposition. Glauber dynamics are used for the spin-flip probability, and the
checkerboard scheme preserves detailed balance, ensuring convergence to the correct
Boltzmann equilibrium. The ferromagnetic phase transition is studied by measuring
the magnetization, internal energy, heat capacity, and magnetic susceptibility as
functions of temperature over the range $T \in [1.5,\, 4.0]$. Results for a
$20\times 20$ lattice are presented and compared with Onsager's exact critical
temperature $T_c = 2/[k_B\ln(1+\sqrt{2})] \approx 2.269$. Peaks in the heat
capacity and susceptibility near $T_c$ confirm a continuous (second-order) phase
transition. Three independent CA update rules --- Glauber, Metropolis-CA, and
Heat Bath --- are compared and shown to yield the same thermodynamic equilibrium.
The CA implementation achieves roughly a 5$\times$ speedup over sequential Monte
Carlo by exploiting the parallelism within each sublattice.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Spontaneous ordering of microscopic degrees of freedom into a macroscopic
collective state is one of the central themes of statistical physics.
The Ising model provides the simplest lattice-based setting in which this
phenomenon --- a second-order phase transition from a disordered paramagnetic
phase to an ordered ferromagnetic phase --- can be studied analytically and
computationally.

The model was proposed by Wilhelm Lenz in 1920 and solved in one dimension by
his student Ernst Ising in 1925, who found no phase transition in 1D. The
far more significant two-dimensional version was solved exactly by Lars Onsager
in 1944, yielding a critical temperature
$T_c = 2J/[k_B\ln(1+\sqrt{2})] \approx 2.269J/k_B$ and demonstrating for
the first time that a short-range interacting system can undergo a true
thermodynamic phase transition.

The significance of the 2D Ising model extends well beyond ferromagnetism.
Its universality class --- characterised by critical exponents
$\beta = 1/8$, $\gamma = 7/4$, and $\alpha = 0$ (logarithmic divergence) ---
describes a wide range of physical and biological systems, from binary alloys
and adsorbed monolayers to neural networks and social dynamics.

Computationally, the model is most commonly simulated by sequential Monte Carlo
(Metropolis algorithm). In this project a \emph{Cellular Automata} (CA) approach
with checkerboard decomposition is used instead. By decomposing the lattice into
two non-interacting sublattices (even and odd sites), all sites in a sublattice
can be updated simultaneously while preserving detailed balance. This retains
the correct equilibrium statistics while offering a substantial speedup over
sequential updating.

The report is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:model} defines the model
and thermodynamic observables. Section~\ref{sec:ca} describes the CA simulation
method and the checkerboard scheme. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents numerical
results for spin configurations, thermodynamic quantities, and a comparison of
update rules. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the results and
finite-size effects. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{The 2D Ising Model}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Hamiltonian}

Consider $N = L \times L$ spins $s_i \in \{-1, +1\}$ arranged on a
two-dimensional square lattice with periodic boundary conditions. The
Hamiltonian is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H} = -J\sum_{\langle i,j\rangle} s_i s_j,
  \label{eq:hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over all nearest-neighbour pairs $\langle i,j\rangle$,
and $J > 0$ is the ferromagnetic coupling constant. With $J > 0$, parallel
spins lower the energy, favouring alignment.

Throughout this work we use \emph{natural units}: $J = 1$ and $k_B = 1$,
so temperature is measured in units of $J/k_B$.

\subsection{Thermodynamic observables}

The key quantities measured in simulation are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Magnetization per spin} (order parameter):
  \begin{equation}
    M = \frac{1}{N}\left|\sum_i s_i\right|.
    \label{eq:mag}
  \end{equation}
  \item \textbf{Energy per spin}:
  \begin{equation}
    E = -\frac{J}{N}\sum_{\langle i,j\rangle} s_i s_j.
    \label{eq:energy}
  \end{equation}
  \item \textbf{Heat capacity} (from energy fluctuations):
  \begin{equation}
    C_V = \frac{\langle E^2\rangle - \langle E\rangle^2}{k_B T^2}.
    \label{eq:cv}
  \end{equation}
  \item \textbf{Magnetic susceptibility} (from magnetization fluctuations):
  \begin{equation}
    \chi = \frac{\langle M^2\rangle - \langle M\rangle^2}{k_B T}.
    \label{eq:chi}
  \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Phase behaviour and critical point}

The 2D Ising model displays three distinct regimes:

\paragraph{Low temperature ($T < T_c$).}
The entropic cost of disorder is small relative to the energy gained by
alignment. Spins order spontaneously into large domains, producing a
non-zero magnetization $M > 0$. The discrete $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry
$s_i \to -s_i$ is spontaneously broken.

\paragraph{Critical point ($T = T_c$).}
The competition between energy and entropy is exactly balanced. The
correlation length diverges, producing scale-invariant fluctuations and
critical opalescence in the spin configuration. Onsager's exact result is:
\begin{equation}
  T_c = \frac{2J}{k_B \ln(1+\sqrt{2})} \approx 2.269\,\frac{J}{k_B}.
  \label{eq:tc}
\end{equation}
The heat capacity diverges logarithmically and the susceptibility diverges
as a power law.

\paragraph{High temperature ($T > T_c$).}
Thermal fluctuations dominate. Spins fluctuate randomly, producing zero
net magnetization and restoring the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry.

\subsection{Critical exponents}

Near $T_c$ the thermodynamic quantities obey power laws:
\begin{equation}
  M \sim |T - T_c|^\beta, \quad C_V \sim \ln|T - T_c|, \quad
  \chi \sim |T - T_c|^{-\gamma},
  \label{eq:exponents}
\end{equation}
with exact values $\beta = 1/8$, $\alpha = 0$ (logarithmic), and
$\gamma = 7/4$. These exponents characterise the universality class of
the 2D Ising model and are shared by all systems in that class regardless
of microscopic details.

\section{Cellular Automata Simulation Method}
\label{sec:ca}

\subsection{Motivation for cellular automata}

In standard sequential Monte Carlo (Metropolis), one spin is chosen at
random and a flip is accepted or rejected according to the Metropolis criterion.
This is inherently sequential: updating one spin changes its neighbours'
local fields, so the next spin must be chosen \emph{after} the previous
update is complete.

Cellular automata offer an alternative: update many spins simultaneously.
The challenge is that naively updating all spins in parallel breaks detailed
balance, since a spin's ``new'' neighbours may have already changed.
The solution is the \emph{checkerboard decomposition}.

\subsection{Checkerboard (red--black) decomposition}

The square lattice is divided into two sublattices based on the parity of
the site index $(i + j)$:
\begin{align}
  \text{Even sublattice (red):} &\quad (i+j) \equiv 0 \pmod{2}, \\
  \text{Odd sublattice (black):} &\quad (i+j) \equiv 1 \pmod{2}.
\end{align}

A key property: nearest neighbours of any even site are \emph{all} odd,
and vice versa. Therefore:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item All even sites can be updated simultaneously --- they do not
    interact with each other.
  \item All odd sites can then be updated simultaneously, seeing the
    updated even sites as fixed.
  \item One \emph{sweep} = even update + odd update $\equiv$ one
    Monte Carlo sweep (in terms of thermodynamic equivalence).
\end{enumerate}

This scheme preserves detailed balance because, at each half-step, every
spin sees a fixed local environment. The method converges to the correct
Boltzmann equilibrium while allowing massive parallelism within each sublattice.

\subsection{Glauber dynamics}

The flip probability for spin $s_i$ under Glauber dynamics is:
\begin{equation}
  P_\text{flip}(s_i) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{\Delta E / k_B T}},
  \label{eq:glauber}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta E = 2s_i h_i$ is the energy cost of flipping $s_i$, and
\begin{equation}
  h_i = J\sum_{\delta} s_{i+\delta}
\end{equation}
is the local field from the four nearest neighbours $\delta \in \{\pm\hat{x}, \pm\hat{y}\}$.
When $\Delta E < 0$ (flip lowers energy), $P_\text{flip} > 1/2$; when
$\Delta E > 0$ (flip raises energy), $P_\text{flip} < 1/2$.

\subsection{Alternative update rules}

Two additional rules are implemented and compared:

\paragraph{Metropolis-CA.}
\begin{equation}
  P_\text{flip} = \min\!\left(1,\, e^{-\Delta E / k_B T}\right).
\end{equation}
This is identical to the sequential Metropolis rule but applied in
parallel within each sublattice.

\paragraph{Heat bath.}
The spin is drawn from its conditional distribution given its neighbours:
\begin{equation}
  P(s_i = +1) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-2h_i / k_B T}}.
\end{equation}
The spin is set to $+1$ with this probability and $-1$ otherwise,
irrespective of its current state.

All three rules satisfy detailed balance (with the checkerboard scheme)
and converge to the same Boltzmann equilibrium, but have different
transient dynamics.

Figure~\ref{fig:spins} illustrates the three qualitative regimes. Below $T_c$,
one spin orientation dominates the lattice, and boundaries between opposite-spin
regions form smooth, compact domains. At the critical point, clusters appear at
all scales, reflecting the diverging correlation length. Above $T_c$, the
configuration is visually indistinguishable from random noise.

\subsection{Thermodynamic quantities}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{fig2_ca_thermodynamic_quantities.png}
  \caption{Thermodynamic quantities as functions of temperature for a
    $20\times 20$ lattice (averaged over 3 independent runs).
    (a) Magnetization: drops from near 1 to near 0 around $T_c$.
    (b) Energy per spin: increases continuously from $\approx -2$ to $\approx -1$.
    (c) Heat capacity: sharp peak near $T_c$.
    (d) Magnetic susceptibility: peak near $T_c$.
    Dashed red vertical lines mark $T_c = 2.269$.}
  \label{fig:thermo}

\subsubsection{Magnetization}
The magnetization (Figure~\ref{fig:thermo}a) serves as the order parameter.
It is close to 1 for $T < 1.8$ and falls sharply near $T_c$, approaching
zero for $T > 3$. The transition is not a sharp discontinuity --- it is a
continuous (second-order) transition, in agreement with the exact solution.
Finite-size effects broaden and slightly shift the apparent transition point
relative to the thermodynamic limit $T_c = 2.269$.

\subsubsection{Energy}
The internal energy per spin (Figure~\ref{fig:thermo}b) changes continuously
across the transition. There is no latent heat, confirming the second-order
character. The energy is approximately $-2J$ at very low temperature (all
spins aligned, each with 4 neighbours contributing $-J$) and approaches
$0$ at high temperature.

\subsubsection{Heat capacity}
The heat capacity (Figure~\ref{fig:thermo}c), computed from energy fluctuations
via Eq.~\eqref{eq:cv}, shows a pronounced peak near $T_c$. In the
thermodynamic limit, $C_V$ diverges logarithmically. For a finite lattice
the peak is finite but shifts and sharpens as $L$ increases.

\subsubsection{Susceptibility}
The magnetic susceptibility (Figure~\ref{fig:thermo}d) also peaks near $T_c$.
Large values of $\chi$ indicate that the magnetization fluctuates strongly ---
a signature of the system being near its critical point where correlation
lengths are long.

\subsection{Summary: order parameter and response function}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig3_ca_combined.png}

\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:combined} overlays the normalized magnetization and heat capacity.
The magnetization drops from 1 to 0 while the heat capacity simultaneously peaks.
This anticorrelation is a general feature of second-order transitions: as the
ordered phase disappears, energy fluctuations become maximal.

Figure~\ref{fig:mag_annotated} presents the clearest view of the phase transition.

\subsection{Comparison of update rules}

with differing dynamics, is a general property of Markov chain Monte Carlo
methods satisfying detailed balance.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Anatomy of the phase transition}

The results in Section~\ref{sec:results} paint a consistent picture. Below
$T_c$ the system is in its ferromagnetic phase: spins align into large
domains, $M \approx 1$, and the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry is spontaneously
broken. The choice of which domain orientation dominates is determined by
the initial conditions (in practice by the first few sweeps).

At $T_c$ the incoherent fixed point loses stability. The correlation length
$\xi$ diverges, meaning fluctuations occur on all spatial scales. This is
visible in Figure~\ref{fig:spins} (centre panel) as a fractal-like domain
structure. The heat capacity and susceptibility peaks arise because critical
fluctuations involve large energy and magnetization excursions.

Above $T_c$ entropy dominates energy. The thermal energy $k_B T$ is
sufficient to flip spins against the local field, destroying long-range order.

\subsection{Finite-size effects}

The simulations use a $20\times 20$ lattice, which introduces finite-size
effects. The most important consequences are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item The apparent transition is rounded: $M$ does not drop discontinuously
    to zero but decreases over a range of temperatures.
  \item The peak of $C_V$ and $\chi$ occurs slightly \emph{below} the
    true $T_c = 2.269$.
  \item The magnetization does not reach exactly zero above $T_c$ because
    finite-sample fluctuations scale as $N^{-1/2}$.
\end{itemize}

These effects would decrease with larger $L$. In the limit $L \to \infty$
the transition becomes sharp at exactly $T_c = 2.269$.

\subsection{CA vs.\ Monte Carlo}

The checkerboard CA is thermodynamically equivalent to sequential MC
(same detailed balance, same equilibrium statistics) but approximately
$5\times$ faster in the present Python implementation. The speedup comes
from replacing $N = 400$ individual random spin selections per sweep with
two vectorised sublattice updates using \texttt{numpy} operations. On a
GPU, the speedup would be far larger, making CA approaches attractive for
large-scale simulations ($L > 1000$).

\subsection{Validity check}

As a consistency check, results from different update rules agree within
statistical error (Figure~\ref{fig:rules}). The energy at low temperature
approaches $E \approx -2J$ per spin, consistent with a fully ordered state
where each spin has 4 aligned neighbours, contributing $-J \times 4/2 = -2J$
per spin (dividing by 2 to avoid double counting). The magnetization at
$T = 1.5$ is $M \approx 0.95$, indicating a nearly fully ordered state, as
expected well below $T_c$.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The 2D Ising model with ferromagnetic nearest-neighbour coupling has been
simulated using a Cellular Automata approach with checkerboard decomposition
and Glauber dynamics. The main findings are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A continuous (second-order) ferromagnetic phase transition is observed
    near $T_c \approx 2.269$, consistent with Onsager's exact result.
  \item The magnetization drops continuously from $M \approx 1$ at low
    temperature to $M \approx 0$ above $T_c$.
  \item The heat capacity and magnetic susceptibility peak near $T_c$,
    confirming enhanced fluctuations at the critical point.
  \item Three CA update rules (Glauber, Metropolis-CA, Heat Bath) converge
    to the same thermodynamic equilibrium, validating the checkerboard scheme.
  \item The CA implementation is approximately $5\times$ faster than sequential
    Monte Carlo for the same lattice size and number of sweeps.
\end{enumerate}

The checkerboard decomposition is a general technique applicable to any
system whose lattice can be bipartitioned into non-interacting sublattices.
It is particularly valuable for GPU computing, where massive parallelism
within each sublattice maps naturally onto hardware architecture.

\section{Simulation Code}
\label{app:code}

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Core class and main simulation loop from \texttt{01\_ising\_cellular\_automata.py}.}]
class IsingCellularAutomata:
    """
    2D Ising Model using Cellular Automata with checkerboard decomposition.
    Checkerboard (red-black) update scheme preserves detailed balance.
    """

    def __init__(self, L=20, T=2.269, rule='glauber'):
        self.L = L; self.T = T; self.N = L * L; self.rule = rule
        self.lattice = np.random.choice([-1, 1], size=(L, L))
        self._create_checkerboard_masks()

    def _create_checkerboard_masks(self):
        i, j = np.indices((self.L, self.L))
        self.mask_even = ((i + j) % 2) == 0
        self.mask_odd  = ((i + j) % 2) == 1

    def local_field(self, lattice):
        return (np.roll(lattice, 1, 0) + np.roll(lattice, -1, 0) +
                np.roll(lattice, 1, 1) + np.roll(lattice, -1, 1))

    def update_sublattice(self, lattice, mask):
        new_lattice = lattice.copy()
        field = self.local_field(lattice)
        spins   = lattice[mask]
        local_h = field[mask]
        dE = 2 * spins * local_h

        if self.rule == 'glauber':
            prob      = 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(dE / self.T))
            flip_mask = np.random.random(len(prob)) < prob
        elif self.rule == 'metropolis_ca':
            flip_mask  = dE < 0
            flip_mask |= np.random.random(len(dE)) < np.exp(-dE / self.T)
        elif self.rule == 'heat_bath':
            prob_up   = 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-2 * local_h / self.T))
            new_spins = np.where(np.random.random(len(prob_up)) < prob_up, 1, -1)
            flip_mask = (new_spins != spins)

        new_lattice[mask] = np.where(flip_mask, -spins, spins)
        return new_lattice

    def step(self):
        # One CA step: update even sublattice, then odd
        self.lattice = self.update_sublattice(self.lattice, self.mask_even)
        self.lattice = self.update_sublattice(self.lattice, self.mask_odd)

    def energy(self):
        E  = -np.sum(self.lattice * np.roll(self.lattice, 1, 0))
        E -= np.sum(self.lattice * np.roll(self.lattice, 1, 1))
        return E / self.N

    def magnetization(self):
        return np.abs(np.sum(self.lattice)) / self.N

    def run(self, eq_steps=500, measure_steps=1000):
        T_c = 2.269
        if abs(self.T - T_c) < 0.3:
            eq_steps = max(eq_steps, 800)   # extra steps near T_c
        for _ in range(eq_steps):
            self.step()
        E_list, M_list = [], []
        for s in range(measure_steps):
            self.step()
            if s % 3 == 0:
                E_list.append(self.energy())
                M_list.append(self.magnetization())
        E_arr, M_arr = np.array(E_list), np.array(M_list)
        C_V = np.var(E_arr, ddof=1) / self.T**2
        chi = np.var(M_arr, ddof=1) / self.T
        return np.mean(E_arr), np.mean(M_arr), C_V, chi

    def run_with_averaging(self, eq_steps=500, measure_steps=1000, n_runs=3):
        results = [[], [], [], []]
        for run in range(n_runs):
            np.random.seed(42 + run * 1000)
            self.lattice = np.random.choice([-1, 1], size=(self.L, self.L))
            for i, v in enumerate(self.run(eq_steps, measure_steps)):
                results[i].append(v)
        return [np.mean(r) for r in results]

# Main temperature scan
L = 20
temps = np.linspace(1.5, 4.0, 14)
data  = {'E': [], 'M': [], 'C': [], 'X': []}

for T in temps:
    model = IsingCellularAutomata(L=L, T=T, rule='glauber')
    E, M, C, X = model.run_with_averaging(
                   eq_steps=500, measure_steps=800, n_runs=3)
    data['E'].append(E); data['M'].append(M)
    data['C'].append(C); data['X'].append(X)
